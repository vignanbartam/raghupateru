What is Forest Conservation

Forest conservation is the practice of protecting and preserving forested areas, their biodiversity, and the ecological services they provide. It involves managing forests in a way that maintains their ecological integrity while also meeting the needs of people who depend on them for their livelihoods. Forest conservation aims to maintain the health of forests and their ecosystems, prevent deforestation, promote sustainable land use practices, and protect biodiversity Forest conservation has three main objectives and they are :-

  • Protecting forested areas from development or conversion to other land uses.
  • Restoring degraded forest areas through reforestation and afforestation.
  • Managing forests in a sustainable way that balances economic, social, and ecological needs.

    • Ex-Situ Conservation

    Ex-situ conservation of biodiversity involves the breeding and maintenance of endangered species in artificial ecosystems such as zoos , nurseries , botanical gardens , gene banks etc. There is less competition for food, water and space among the organisms.

    Ex-situ conservation has the following advantages:

    1. The animals are provided with a longer time and breeding activity.
    2. The species bred in captivity can be reintroduced in the wild.
    3. Genetic techniques can be used for the preservation of endangered species.

    EDGE Species

    Evolutionarily Distinct and Globally Endangered (EDGE) species are animal species which have a high 'EDGE score', a metric combining endangered conservation status with the genetic distinctiveness of the particular taxon. Distinctive species have few closely related species, and EDGE species are often the only surviving member of their genus or even higher taxonomic rank. The extinction of such species would therefore represent a disproportionate loss of unique evolutionary history and biodiversity.
    Ex : Pandas , Elephants , Purple Frog etc
